# Indonesian Spice Knowledge Base — Complete Reference

## 1. CINNAMON — Cassia vs. Ceylon (Sri Lankan)

### Cassia Cinnamon (Indonesian / Padang Cassia)
- **Scientific name**: Cinnamomum burmannii (Indonesian Cassia / Padang Cassia)
- Also called: Korintje cinnamon, Indonesian cassia, Batavia cassia
- **Origin**: Primarily West Sumatra (Padang region), also Java
- **HS Code**: 0906.11 (cinnamon & cinnamon-tree flowers, neither crushed nor ground)
- **Key characteristics**:
  - Thicker bark (2–3 mm), single layer, harder and rougher texture
  - Reddish-brown color, strong, bold, slightly harsh cinnamon flavor
  - High coumarin content: 1,000–12,200 mg/kg (depending on grade)
  - Lower essential oil: ~1–2.5%
  - Cheaper than Ceylon, widely used in baked goods and processed food
  - Dominant in US market; sold as "cinnamon" in most American supermarkets

### Ceylon Cinnamon (True Cinnamon / Sri Lankan Cinnamon)
- **Scientific name**: Cinnamomum verum (syn. Cinnamomum zeylanicum)
- Also called: True cinnamon, Mexican cinnamon (when imported via Mexico)
- **Origin**: Sri Lanka (primary), also Southern India, Madagascar, Brazil
- **HS Code**: 0906.11
- **Key characteristics**:
  - Very thin bark layers (< 0.5 mm), rolled into multi-layer "quills"
  - Light tan/buff color, complex sweet flavor with subtle citrus and clove notes
  - Very LOW coumarin: < 0.04 mg/kg (EU-safe for daily consumption)
  - Higher essential oil: ~2–4%, contains more eugenol
  - Premium grade, more expensive; preferred in Europe and health-conscious markets
  - EU regulations (EFSA) favor Ceylon due to safe coumarin levels

### Cassia vs. Ceylon — Key Differences Summary
| Feature | Cassia (Indonesian) | Ceylon (Sri Lankan) |
|---------|--------------------|--------------------|
| Scientific name | C. burmannii | C. verum |
| Bark thickness | Thick (2–3 mm) | Thin multi-layer quill |
| Coumarin content | Very HIGH (1,000–12,200 mg/kg) | Very LOW (< 0.04 mg/kg) |
| Flavor | Bold, strong, slightly harsh | Delicate, sweet, complex |
| Price | Lower | Higher (premium) |
| EU compliance | Restricted (coumarin limits) | Fully compliant |
| Main market | USA, Asia | EU, health segment |

### Indonesian Cassia Grades (Padang Cassia)
- **AABB**: Best quality, uniform thickness, minimal defects
- **AA**: High quality, standard commercial grade
- **A**: Medium quality
- **C/D**: Lower grades, more cracks, mixed pieces

## 2. NUTMEG — Banda Nutmeg vs. Papuan Nutmeg

### Banda Nutmeg (True Nutmeg)
- **Scientific name**: Myristica fragrans
- Also called: Common nutmeg, true nutmeg, commercial nutmeg
- **Origin**: Banda Islands, North Maluku Province, Indonesia (primary world source)
  Also: Grenada (West Indies), Malaysia, India (Kerala)
- **HS Code**: 0908.11 (nutmeg, neither crushed nor ground)
- **Key characteristics**:
  - Produces BOTH nutmeg (seed kernel) AND mace (aril covering the seed)
  - Oval-shaped, about 2–3 cm long
  - Strong, warm, sweet-spicy aroma with complex flavor
  - Essential oil: 5–15%, contains myristicin, elemicin, safrole
  - Commercial standard: 80–85 nuts per 100g ("ABCD" or "SSL" grades)
  - Indonesia produces ~75% of world's nutmeg supply
  - North Maluku, South Maluku, and Papua are main production areas

### Papuan Nutmeg (Papua / New Guinea Nutmeg)
- **Scientific name**: Myristica argentea
- Also called: New Guinea nutmeg, long nutmeg, macassar nutmeg
- **Origin**: Papua (Indonesian Papua Province), Papua New Guinea
- **HS Code**: 0908.19 (other nutmeg)
- **Key characteristics**:
  - Elongated shape (longer than M. fragrans), smaller diameter
  - Lighter, less complex flavor and aroma compared to Banda nutmeg
  - Lower essential oil content; lower myristicin
  - Lower market value; sometimes used as adulterant or substituted for true nutmeg
  - Does NOT produce commercially valued mace
  - Produced in interior Papua regions

### Banda vs. Papuan Nutmeg — Key Differences Summary
| Feature | Banda Nutmeg | Papuan Nutmeg |
|---------|-------------|---------------|
| Scientific name | Myristica fragrans | Myristica argentea |
| Shape | Oval/round | Elongated |
| Origin | Banda Islands (Maluku) | Papua / New Guinea |
| Aroma | Rich, warm, complex | Milder, less complex |
| Mace production | YES (commercial) | NO (not commercial grade) |
| Essential oil | Higher (5–15%) | Lower |
| Market price | Higher | Lower |
| HS Code | 0908.11 | 0908.19 |
| Commercial use | Primary spice trade | Substitute / lower-grade |

### Nutmeg Grades (Banda)
- **ABCD**: All sizes, mixed; most common export grade
- **SS** (Shrivelled Sorts): Premium whole, well-dried
- **BWP** (Broken, Wormy, Punky): Lower grade
- **Mace grades**: #1 (premium), #2 (commercial)

## 3. BLACK PEPPER

- **Scientific name**: Piper nigrum
- **HS Code**: 0904.11 (neither crushed nor ground)
- **Key origins in Indonesia**: Bangka Island (Bangka Belitung Province), Lampung (Sumatra)
- **Types**:
  - **Black pepper**: Dried unripe green berries; most pungent
  - **White pepper**: Ripe berries, outer skin removed; milder, cleaner taste
  - Bangka white pepper (Muntok white pepper): World's highest quality white pepper
- **Grades**: ASTA (American Spice Trade Association), FAQ (Fair Average Quality)
- **Piperine content**: 5–9% (determines pungency)

---

## 4. WHITE PEPPER

- **Scientific name**: Piper nigrum (same plant as black pepper)
- **HS Code**: 0904.12 (white pepper, neither crushed nor ground)
- **Bangka Muntok White Pepper**: Premium grade, world benchmark
- **Characteristics**: Milder, earthy, cleaner aroma vs. black pepper
- **Export grades**: ASTA 1.0 / 0.5 (moisture, light berries, filth standards)

---

## 5. CLOVE

- **Scientific name**: Syzygium aromaticum
- **HS Code**: 0907.10
- **Origin**: Maluku (historical "Spice Islands"), Sulawesi, Zanzibar
- **Types**: Whole clove buds, stems (syzygium), ground clove
- **Eugenol content**: 70–90% in essential oil; primary active compound
- **Indonesia**: World's largest producer AND consumer (clove cigarettes / kretek)
- **Grades**: Hand-picked grade A, FAQ, Grade B

---

## 6. VANILLA

- **Scientific name**: Vanilla planifolia (Bourbon/Tahitian: V. tahitensis)
- **HS Code**: 0905.10 (neither crushed nor ground)
- **Key origins**: Madagascar, Indonesia (Sulawesi, Bali, Papua), Tahiti
- **Indonesian vanilla**: Known for creamy, sweet, woody flavor profile
- **Grades**: Grade A (moisture > 25%, premium), Grade B (baking grade)
- **Vanillin content**: 1.5–2.5% (determines quality)
- **Price**: Among the most expensive spices globally (supply-demand volatility)

---

## 7. CASSIA POWDER

- **Scientific name**: Cinnamomum burmannii
- **HS Code**: 0906.19 (crushed or ground)
- **Same species as cassia whole** but ground/milled
- Higher coumarin exposure when consumed in quantity
- Common in seasoning blends, instant food, bakery applications

---

## 8. PATCHOULI OIL

- **Scientific name**: Pogostemon cablin
- **HS Code**: 3301.29 (essential oils)
- **Origin**: Aceh (North Sumatra), Sulawesi, Java
- **Indonesia**: Produces ~85% of world's patchouli oil
- **Uses**: Perfumery, cosmetics, aromatherapy, incense, fixative
- **Characteristics**: Deep, earthy, musky, sweet-herbal scent
- **Grades**: Light (young leaves), Dark (aged), Indonesian standard ISO 3757
- **Patchoulol content**: min 30% in premium grade

---

## 9. MACE

- **Scientific name**: Myristica fragrans (same plant as Banda nutmeg)
- **HS Code**: 0908.21 (mace, neither crushed nor ground)
- **Description**: Bright red lacy aril (seed covering) of the nutmeg fruit
- **Origin**: Same as Banda nutmeg — North Maluku, Indonesia
- **Flavor**: Similar to nutmeg but more delicate, slightly sweeter and warmer
- **Grades**: #1 (premium, full blades), #2 (commercial, broken blades), BWP

## QUALITY CERTIFICATIONS FOR INDONESIAN SPICE EXPORTS

- **COA** (Certificate of Analysis): Moisture, purity, essential oil %, microbial limits
- **COO** (Certificate of Origin): Issued by KADIN (Chamber of Commerce) or Ministry of Trade
- **Phytosanitary Certificate**: Required by most importing countries; issued by BSIP (Agency for Agricultural Quarantine)
- **HALAL Certificate**: MUI (Majelis Ulama Indonesia)
- **Organic Certificate**: LSPO or equivalent (BioInspecta, Control Union)
- **Food Safety**: BPOM approval for processed products

---

## COMMON INDONESIAN SPICE EXPORT MISTAKES TO AVOID

1. Confusing Myristica fragrans (Banda nutmeg) with Myristica argentea (Papuan nutmeg) in documents
2. Using the wrong HS code (0908.11 vs 0908.19 for nutmeg varieties)
3. Not disclosing coumarin content in cassia exports to EU — EFSA Regulation EC 1334/2008
4. Failing to separate mace from nutmeg HS codes (0908.21 vs 0908.11)
5. Missing Phytosanitary Certificate from BSIP for plant-based spices
